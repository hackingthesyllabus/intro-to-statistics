Learning Statistics

A decision flowchart for choosing confidence intervals, z tests, t tests, and chi square tests.

How to use this

Start at the top. First decide whether you are estimating or testing. Then identify whether the outcome variable is numeric or categorical. Then choose the formula that matches the structure of the question.

Are you estimating a population value or testing a hypothesis?

If you are estimating

Use a confidence interval.

If you are testing

Identify the type of outcome variable.

Confidence interval for a mean

Use when the outcome is numeric and you want to estimate a population mean.

Ȳ ± t* (sY / √N)

Example cue: “Construct a 95% confidence interval for the average burnout score.”

Confidence interval for a proportion

Use when the outcome is categorical and you want to estimate a population proportion.

p̂ ± z* √(p̂(1 − p̂) / n)

Use when the question asks you to estimate the proportion and give a confidence interval.

What kind of outcome variable do you have?

Numeric outcome = mean, average, score, years

Categorical outcome = proportion, percent, yes or no, counts

Numeric outcome

Trigger words: mean, average, score, years

One sample t test

One sample mean compared with a known or hypothesized population mean.

t = (Ȳ − μ0) / (sY / √N)

Example cue: “Is the average number of years of education significantly different from 12.2?”

Two sample t test

Compare the means of two groups.

t = (Ȳ1 − Ȳ2) / SEȲ₁ − Ȳ₂

Example cue: “Is there a gender difference in job satisfaction?”

Tail check for t tests

“Different” means two tailed.

“Greater than” or “less than” means one tailed.

Categorical outcome

Trigger words: proportion, percent, yes or no, counts

One proportion z test

One sample proportion compared with a known or hypothesized population proportion.

z = (p̂ − p0) / √(p0(1 − p0) / n)

Example cue: “Is the poverty rate less than 5 percent?”

Two proportion z test

Compare proportions for two groups.

z = (p̂1 − p̂2) / SEp̂₁ − p̂₂

Example cue: “Is there a gender difference in treatment seeking?”

Chi square test of association

Use when both variables are categorical and the data are shown in a table of counts.

χ² = Σ ((O − E)² / E)
Expected count: E = (row total × column total) / grand total

Example cue: “Is there an association between robbery victimization and concern about crime?”

Final step: interpret the result

  • If p < α, reject H0.
  • If p ≥ α, fail to reject H0.
  • For a confidence interval, say: “We are 95 percent confident that the true value lies between ... and ...”
  • For tests, say: “There is a statistically significant difference ...” or “There is not a statistically significant difference ...”
  • For chi square, say: “There is evidence of an association ...” or “There is not evidence of an association ...”

Quick recognition guide

  • Mean / average / score / years usually points to t procedures.
  • Proportion / percent / yes-no usually points to z procedures.
  • Association / relationship / table of counts usually points to chi square.
  • Estimate means confidence interval.
  • Different usually means two tailed.
  • Greater than or less than usually means one tailed.

Important reminder

Do not start by hunting for a formula. Start by identifying what kind of quantity you have and what the question is asking you to do. The formula comes after that.

    Start

    Are you estimating a population value or testing a hypothesis?

    Estimate

    Use a confidence interval. Then ask whether the outcome is a mean or a proportion.

    Confidence interval for a mean

    \[ar{Y} \pm t^* s_{ar{Y}}\]
    \[s_{ar{Y}} = rac{s_Y}{\sqrt{N}}, \quad df = N - 1\]

    Confidence interval for a proportion

    \[\hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{ rac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\]

    Test

    Identify the outcome variable. Numeric outcomes use t procedures. Categorical outcomes use z procedures or chi square.

    Numeric outcome

    Mean, average, score, years. Next ask whether you have one sample or two.

    One sample t test

    \[t = rac{ar{Y} - \mu_0}{s_{ar{Y}}}\]
    \[s_{ar{Y}} = rac{s_Y}{\sqrt{N}}\]

    Two sample t test

    \[t = rac{ar{Y}_1 - ar{Y}_2}{s_{ar{Y}_1 - ar{Y}_2}}\]
    \[s_{ar{Y}_1 - ar{Y}_2} = \sqrt{\left(s_{ar{Y}_1} ight)^2 + \left(s_{ar{Y}_2} ight)^2}\]

    Categorical outcome

    Proportion, percent, yes or no, rate. Next ask whether both variables are categorical.

    One proportion or two proportions?

    One sample compared to a known value uses one proportion z. Two groups uses two proportion z.

    One proportion z test

    \[z = rac{\hat{p} - \pi_0}{s_{\pi_0}}\]
    \[s_{\pi_0} = \sqrt{ rac{\pi_0(1-\pi_0)}{n}}\]

    Two proportion z test

    \[z = rac{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2}{SE_{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2}}\]
    \[SE_{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2} = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left( rac{1}{n_1}+ rac{1}{n_2} ight)}\]

    Chi square test of association

    \[\chi^2 = \sum rac{(f_o-f_e)^2}{f_e}\]
    \[f_e = rac{( ext{column total})( ext{row total})}{n}, \quad df = (r-1)(c-1)\]
